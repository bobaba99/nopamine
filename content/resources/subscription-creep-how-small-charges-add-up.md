## The Number You're Probably Wrong About

If someone asked you how much you spend on subscriptions each month, what would you say? $50? $80? Research from C+R Research (2022) found that the average American spends around $219 per month on subscriptions — but when surveyed, most people estimated they spent about $86. That's a gap of roughly $133 per month, or nearly $1,600 per year, quietly leaving your account for services you may barely use.

This isn't forgetfulness. It's a well-documented psychological pattern. Subscription models are designed to exploit what behavioral economists call "status quo bias" — our strong preference for leaving things as they are. Once a subscription is set up, canceling it requires active effort, and our brains treat that effort as a loss. So we do nothing.

## Why We Keep Subscriptions We Don't Use

Three cognitive patterns work together to keep unused subscriptions alive:

**Sunk cost reasoning.** "I've already paid for six months — I should get my money's worth." But past payments are gone regardless. The real question is whether the *next* month's payment is worth it. Research from *Advances in Consumer Research* (2024) found that sunk-cost reminders only modestly predict actual continued use (r = 0.41), meaning people stay subscribed without genuinely engaging.

**Optimistic forecasting.** "I'll definitely start using the gym / meditation app / language course next month." We consistently overestimate our future engagement with services. A 2024 study on subscription psychology found that consumers predict 40-60% more usage than they actually deliver in the following quarter.

**Decision fatigue avoidance.** Evaluating whether to keep each subscription requires mental effort. When you have 8-12 active subscriptions, the cognitive load of auditing them feels overwhelming — so you defer it indefinitely.

## A Simple Audit Method

Set aside 15 minutes and go through your bank or credit card statement for the last two months. For each recurring charge, answer one question: "Did I actively use this in the last two weeks?"

Not "could I use it" or "might I use it" — did you actually use it? If the answer is no for two consecutive months, that subscription is costing you money without returning value.

A useful benchmark: if you're paying for something monthly, it should be delivering value at least weekly. A $15/month service used four times a month costs $3.75 per use — reasonable. Used once a month, it's $15 per use — and you should ask whether a pay-per-use alternative exists.

## Precommitted Rules

One of the most effective strategies from behavioral research is setting rules *before* you encounter the decision. For subscriptions, a precommitted rule might be: "Any subscription I haven't used in the last 14 days gets canceled. I can always re-subscribe later."

This works because it removes the in-the-moment deliberation. The rule decides for you, which sidesteps decision fatigue entirely.
