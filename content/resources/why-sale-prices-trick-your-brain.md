## The Anchor Effect

When you see a jacket marked down from $200 to $120, your brain doesn't process "$120 jacket." It processes "$80 saved." The original price acts as an "anchor" — a reference point that makes the sale price feel like a gain rather than a cost.

Tversky and Kahneman (1974) first identified anchoring as a core cognitive bias, and decades of replication have confirmed its power in pricing contexts. Ariely et al. (2003, *Quarterly Journal of Economics*) demonstrated that even clearly arbitrary anchors — like the last two digits of your social security number — influence how much people are willing to pay for products. When the anchor is a "was" price on a sale tag, the effect is even stronger because it feels relevant and informative.

The result: sales don't just make you think a product is cheaper. They make you feel like you're *gaining* something — and gains are powerfully motivating.

## The "Transaction Utility" Illusion

Economist Richard Thaler coined the term "transaction utility" to describe the pleasure people get from feeling like they've scored a deal — separate from the actual value of the product. In a classic study, Thaler (1985) showed that people were willing to pay more for a beer bought from a fancy hotel than from a run-down grocery store, even when the beer was identical. The perceived "deal" from the context shifted their willingness to pay.

Sale prices amplify transaction utility. You're not just buying a product; you're "winning" a discount. This feels good — and that good feeling can override the more important question: "Do I actually need or want this at the sale price?"

A useful test: would you buy the item at the sale price if there were no original price shown? If a store simply listed the jacket at $120 with no "was $200" reference, would you still want it? If the answer is no, the discount — not the product — is what's driving the decision.

## Manufactured Urgency

Sales are almost always time-limited ("ends Sunday!"), quantity-limited ("only 3 left!"), or both. This isn't coincidental. Scarcity and urgency trigger what psychologists call "reactance" — a heightened desire for something that feels like it might become unavailable.

Aggarwal et al. (2011, *Journal of Consumer Research*) found that purchase-quantity limits ("limit 12 per customer") increased sales by 30-100% even when the limits were far above what any individual would buy. The mere presence of a limit signals scarcity, and scarcity signals value — whether or not the scarcity is real.

Combined with anchoring and transaction utility, manufactured urgency creates a triple push: the price feels like a gain, the product feels valuable because it's scarce, and the deadline eliminates time for deliberation.

## How to Reclaim Your Evaluation

Three research-backed strategies can help you evaluate sale purchases more clearly:

**Remove the anchor mentally.** Cover or ignore the original price and evaluate the product at the sale price alone. Ask: "Is this item worth $120 to me?" Not "Am I saving $80?" This simple reframe shifts your evaluation from transaction utility to actual utility.

**Apply the cooling-off period.** If a sale lasts 48 hours, you have time to wait 24. If a "flash sale" gives you only an hour, that's a pressure tactic — and purchases made under time pressure have the highest regret rates. Real value doesn't expire.

**Calculate cost-per-use at the sale price.** A 40% discount on something you'll use once is still a low-value purchase. A 10% discount on something you'll use daily might be excellent. The discount percentage is irrelevant; the value-per-use is what matters.
