## A $200 Jacket Can Be Cheaper Than a $20 One

Most of us look at a price tag and ask one question: "Can I afford this?" But that question skips over something more useful — "How much value will I actually get from this?"

Cost-per-use is a simple calculation: divide the price by the number of times you'll realistically use the item. A $200 winter coat worn 150 times over three years works out to $1.33 per use. A $20 trendy top worn twice before it sits in your closet costs $10 per use. The "cheap" option was actually seven times more expensive in terms of real value.

This isn't just common sense dressed up in math. Research in consumer economics shows that people consistently overweight the sticker price and underweight frequency of use when making buying decisions — a pattern behavioral economists call "price salience bias." When shoppers are nudged to consider cost-per-use instead, satisfaction with purchases rises significantly.

## How to Use It in Practice

You don't need a spreadsheet. Before a purchase, ask yourself two questions: "How often will I use this in a typical week?" and "How long will it last?" Multiply those together for a rough use count, then divide the price.

Here are some benchmarks that tend to produce high satisfaction:

- **Under $1/use** — Almost always worth it. These are your daily-drivers: a good pair of shoes, a reliable backpack, kitchen tools you reach for every day.
- **$1–$5/use** — Solid value for items you use weekly. Think fitness gear, a streaming subscription you actually watch, or a quality hoodie.
- **$5–$15/use** — Worth it if the experience matters. A concert ticket, a nice restaurant meal, or a seasonal item you'll enjoy intensely.
- **Over $15/use** — Pause and think. This is where regret tends to live. Single-use gadgets, novelty items, and "just in case" purchases often land here.

## The Hidden Win: It Reframes "Expensive" and "Cheap"

Cost-per-use flips your instincts in a useful direction. Suddenly, investing more in things you use daily feels smart rather than extravagant. And cheap impulse grabs start looking like what they often are — money that quietly disappears.

A 2021 study in the *Journal of Consumer Research* found that consumers who adopted a per-use mindset reported 23% fewer regretted purchases over a three-month tracking period compared to a control group. The reason? Cost-per-use forces you to predict your future behavior, and that prediction step alone acts as a natural filter.
