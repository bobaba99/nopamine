## The Problem With Stars

Online reviews are one of the most powerful forces in modern purchasing decisions. A 2021 survey by PowerReviews found that 99.9% of online shoppers read reviews at least sometimes, and 79% specifically seek them out before buying. We rely on them heavily — but the system has some serious cracks.

A 2020 study published in the *Journal of Marketing* by He et al. found that an estimated 30-40% of online reviews on major platforms show signs of manipulation — including incentivized reviews, review farms, and AI-generated content. The problem has only grown since. Meanwhile, a study by Luca (2016, *Harvard Business School*) showed that even a one-star difference in average rating can shift revenue by 5-9% for businesses. The incentive to manipulate is enormous.

For you as a buyer, this means the review landscape is a mix of genuine opinions, paid promotions, and outright fabrications — and they all look the same at first glance.

## Signals That a Review Is Genuine

Research into review authenticity has identified several patterns that help separate real feedback from noise:

**Moderate-length reviews with specific details.** Genuine reviewers tend to mention specific use cases, timeframes, and comparisons to alternatives. Fake reviews tend toward either very short ("Great product!") or very long, vaguely enthusiastic text. Ott et al. (2011, *Computational Linguistics*) found that deceptive reviews use more superlatives and fewer concrete nouns.

**Verified purchase indicators.** Reviews tied to a confirmed purchase are statistically more reliable — though not immune to manipulation. Prioritize platforms that surface this signal clearly.

**The "three-star zone."** Three- and four-star reviews are disproportionately honest. They come from people who actually used the product and had a mixed, realistic experience. Extreme reviews (one-star and five-star) are the most likely to be fake or emotionally charged. Reading the middle of the distribution gives you the most accurate picture.

**Temporal patterns.** A sudden spike in five-star reviews over a short period often indicates a review campaign. Genuine reviews accumulate steadily over time.

## Signals That a Review Is Unreliable

**Reviewer received the product for free.** Many platforms require disclosure, but it's often buried. Incentivized reviewers rate products 0.38 stars higher on average (Mayzlin et al., 2014, *American Economic Review*).

**Emotional language without specifics.** "This changed my life!" with no concrete explanation is a red flag. Genuine enthusiasm usually comes with context.

**Copy-paste patterns.** If multiple reviews use identical or near-identical phrasing, that's a review farm signal. This is surprisingly common and easy to spot once you look for it.

## A Practical Review-Reading Method

Instead of scanning the star average, try this approach: sort by "most recent," read five to eight reviews in the three-to-four-star range, and look for recurring themes. If three separate reviewers mention that the battery life is shorter than advertised, that's a reliable signal. If one reviewer complains about a unique shipping issue, that's noise.

The recurring themes across moderate reviews are your best estimate of the product's actual performance.
