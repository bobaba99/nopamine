## The Treadmill You Didn't Know You Were On

In 1971, psychologists Brickman and Campbell coined the term "hedonic treadmill" to describe a striking pattern: people return to a baseline level of happiness after both positive and negative life events. Lottery winners, within a year of their windfall, reported happiness levels roughly comparable to where they started.

The same principle applies at the scale of everyday purchases. That new phone that felt magical for the first week? Within a month, it's just your phone. The fresh sneakers that made you walk taller? Three weeks later, they're just shoes. This isn't ingratitude — it's hedonic adaptation, and it's one of the most replicated findings in psychology.

A meta-analysis by Luhmann et al. (2012, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*) confirmed that adaptation to material goods happens faster and more completely than most people predict. We overestimate how long a new purchase will make us happy by a wide margin — a phenomenon Daniel Gilbert calls "impact bias."

## Why It Matters for Spending

Hedonic adaptation creates a cycle: you buy something, it feels great, the feeling fades, you buy the next thing to recapture it. This isn't a personal failing. It's a feature of how your brain processes novelty — new stimuli get more neural attention, and familiar stimuli get less. Your brain is literally wired to stop noticing things it has already categorized as "known."

The practical consequence is that chasing satisfaction through frequent purchases tends to produce diminishing returns. Each new item delivers a shorter burst of excitement than the last, because your baseline expectation keeps rising.

## What Resists Adaptation

Not all purchases fade at the same rate. Research by Van Boven and Gilovich (2003, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*) found that experiential purchases — trips, classes, events, meals with friends — produce longer-lasting satisfaction than material purchases. The reason? Experiences are harder to adapt to because they're unique, they become part of your identity narrative, and they improve in memory over time (while material goods physically degrade).

Within material purchases, items that enable *ongoing experiences* resist adaptation better than static objects. A good kitchen knife that you cook with daily sustains its value because the experience of using it varies. A decorative item that just sits on a shelf fades fast.

Other adaptation-resistant categories include purchases that:

- **Involve skill development** — instruments, art supplies, sports equipment you actively use
- **Facilitate social connection** — board games, hosting supplies, shared subscriptions
- **Remove a recurring irritation** — a comfortable mattress, noise-canceling headphones for a loud commute, a reliable tool replacing a broken one

## A Practical Filter

Before a purchase, ask: "Will this still be adding something to my day-to-day life in three months?" If the honest answer is "probably not — the novelty will wear off," that's a signal to reconsider, not a reason to feel guilty, but a reason to redirect your money toward something with more staying power.
