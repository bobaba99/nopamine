## We're Surprisingly Bad at Predicting This

You'd think you'd be the world's leading expert on what makes you happy. But a large body of research in "affective forecasting" — the study of how people predict their future emotions — shows that we're systematically off.

Gilbert and Wilson (2007, *Science*) found that people routinely overestimate the intensity and duration of their emotional reactions to future events, including purchases. We think the new couch will make us 8/10 happy for six months. In reality, it makes us 6/10 happy for three weeks, then fades into the background. This "impact bias" is one of the most robust findings in happiness research, replicated across cultures and product categories.

The good news: while our intuitive predictions are unreliable, the research has identified patterns that reliably predict post-purchase satisfaction. You don't need to guess — you can check against these patterns.

## Four Predictors of Lasting Satisfaction

**It solves a real, recurring friction.** Purchases that eliminate an ongoing annoyance produce some of the highest and most durable satisfaction. Dunn et al. (2011, *Journal of Consumer Psychology*) found that spending money to save time or reduce daily hassles consistently outperforms spending on luxury goods. A better mattress for back pain, a dishwasher that replaces hand-washing, noise-canceling headphones for a noisy commute — these keep delivering value because the problem they solve keeps recurring.

**You'll use it frequently.** Frequency of use is one of the strongest predictors of purchase satisfaction. A 2019 study in *Psychological Science* by Whillans et al. found that purchases integrated into daily routines produced more sustained well-being than infrequent-use items of equal or greater cost. The math is intuitive: more use means more moments of value, and more moments of value means slower hedonic adaptation.

**It aligns with your values, not just your wants.** Research on "self-concordant" purchases — those that align with your deeper goals and identity — shows they produce longer-lasting satisfaction. Spending $300 on cooking equipment when you genuinely value home cooking and want to get better at it will satisfy differently than spending $300 on a gadget you saw in an ad and wanted in the moment. The difference is that value-aligned purchases don't need novelty to sustain their appeal.

**It's not primarily for social comparison.** Purchases motivated by keeping up with others — the latest phone because your friend has it, the brand-name item for the status it signals — tend to produce shorter-lived satisfaction. Solnick and Hemenway (1998) demonstrated that positional goods (items valued mainly because others don't have them) are inherently fragile: their value depends on a comparison that keeps shifting.

## A Pre-Purchase Checklist

Before buying, run through these four questions:

1. Does this solve a problem I encounter at least weekly?
2. Will I use this at least a few times per month for the next six months?
3. Does this connect to something I care about beyond the purchase itself?
4. Am I buying this for me, or because of how it looks to others?

You don't need a perfect score. But if the answer to most of these is "no," the research suggests your satisfaction will be short-lived — and your money could go further elsewhere.
